- Player 1 (you) wins if both players choose the same action (A0, A0 or A1, A1).

$$E = 4pq - 2p - 2q + 1$$
$$E = (2p-1)(2q-1)$$

This is **a coordination game with a twist**: the payoff is **+1 if both players choose the same action, -1 if they choose different actions**.

But wait: the other player’s payoff is exactly the opposite of yours in each case — it’s antisymmetric.

So in every outcome:
- Your payoff = - (Other player’s payoff)

This is a **zero-sum game** with payoff matrix (in terms of your payoff):

|          | P1: A0 | P1: A1 |
|----------|--------|--------|
| **You: A0** | +1     | -1     |
| **You: A1** | -1     | +1     |

So this is a **2x2 zero-sum game**, and the payoff matrix for Player 2 (you) is:

```
        P1 A0   P1 A1
You A0   +1     -1
You A1   -1     +1
```

---

### Step 2: Find the Nash Equilibrium

In a two-player zero-sum game, the optimal strategy is to play a mixed strategy that makes the opponent indifferent between their actions.

Let’s assume:
- You (Player 2) play A0 with probability `p`, and A1 with probability `1 - p`.
- The other player (Player 1) plays A0 with probability `q`, and A1 with probability `1 - q`.

We want to find your optimal `p` such that no matter what the other player does, you can’t do better, and ideally, you equalize the expected payoff.

But since the game is symmetric (and zero-sum), we can find the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium.

Let’s compute the expected payoff for you (Player 2) as a function of your strategy `p`, assuming the other player plays `q`.

Expected payoff to you:
- If P1 plays A0 (prob q):  
  Your expected payoff = p*(+1) + (1−p)*(−1) = p − (1−p) = 2p − 1
- If P1 plays A1 (prob 1−q):  
  Your expected payoff = p*(−1) + (1−p)*(+1) = -p + (1−p) = 1 − 2p

So overall expected payoff:
E = q*(2p − 1) + (1−q)*(1 − 2p)

Let’s expand:
E = q(2p − 1) + (1−q)(1 − 2p)  
= 2pq − q + (1 − 2p − q + 2pq)  
Wait — better to expand directly:

= q(2p − 1) + (1−q)(1 − 2p)  
= 2pq − q + (1 − 2p) − q(1 − 2p)  
= 2pq − q + 1 − 2p − q + 2pq  
Wait — this is messy. Let's just expand:

(1−q)(1 − 2p) = 1(1 − 2p) − q(1 − 2p) = 1 − 2p − q + 2pq

So total:
E = (2pq − q) + (1 − 2p − q + 2pq)  
= 2pq − q + 1 − 2p − q + 2pq  
= 4pq − 2q − 2p + 1

So E = 4pq − 2p − 2q + 1

Now, in zero-sum games, the optimal strategy for you is to choose `p` such that the expected payoff is **maximin**: you want to maximize the minimum expected payoff.

But more directly: for the equilibrium, we want to choose `p` such that the opponent is indifferent between A0 and A1, so they cannot exploit your strategy.

Let’s suppose you choose `p`. What is the expected payoff if P1 plays A0?

E_A0 = p*(+1) + (1−p)*(−1) = 2p − 1

If P1 plays A1:
E_A1 = p*(−1) + (1−p)*(+1) = −p + 1 − p = 1 − 2p

To make P1 indifferent between A0 and A1, set:
2p − 1 = 1 − 2p  
=> 2p + 2p = 1 + 1  
=> 4p = 2  
=> p = 0.5

So if you play A0 with probability 0.5, then P1 is indifferent between A0 and A1 — they cannot gain by deviating.

Similarly, if P1 plays 0.5, your best response is p = 0.5.

Thus, the **unique Nash equilibrium** is both players playing A0 and A1 with 50% probability.

---

### Step 3: Why is this optimal?

In this equilibrium, your expected payoff is:
- When you play p = 0.5:
  - If P1 plays A0: E = 2*(0.5) − 1 = 0
  - If P1 plays A1: E = 1 − 2*(0.5) = 0
- So regardless of P1’s strategy, your expected payoff is 0.

And since it’s a zero-sum game, you cannot expect to get more than 0 in expectation in equilibrium (the opponent will prevent it).

Thus, to **maximize your minimum possible expected payoff** (the robust strategy), the best you can do is play p = 0.5.
